## Executive Summary

Bret Taylor — co-founder of Sierra, chairman of the OpenAI board, and one of Silicon Valley's most credentialed operators (Google Maps co-creator, Facebook CTO, Salesforce co-CEO) — sits down with Stripe's John Collison for a wide-ranging conversation about the current state and near-future trajectory of AI.

The central thesis running through the interview is that **AI's real economic value lies not in replacing people but in reimagining business processes end-to-end**. Taylor argues that companies are making a critical mistake by deploying AI department-by-department (giving everyone Copilot and calling it "AI transformation") rather than identifying discrete, measurable business processes — like supplier onboarding or commercial contracting — and optimizing them holistically with AI agents.

On the **software engineering front**, Taylor describes a world already in transition: coding agents have improved so dramatically in the last four months that most Silicon Valley companies will stop writing code by hand in 2026. The implications ripple outward — documentation may become more valuable than code itself, "harness engineering" (building the scaffolding around AI agents) is emerging as a key discipline, and a new class of **hyper-generalist "product engineers"** who combine taste, customer empathy, and technical breadth are becoming exponentially more valuable.

Taylor is **bullish on applied AI** but cautious about hype. He believes there are "trillions of dollars of economic value" yet to be realized even if model development paused today, but the bottleneck is the immaturity of the applied AI market — there simply aren't enough companies building agents for boring-but-important business processes. He's notably **skeptical of MCP** as a long-term architecture, arguing that rich contextual approaches (like markdown files and repo-like structures) produce better agents than multi-agent systems with thin protocol layers.

On **Sierra's business**, the numbers are striking: $100M ARR in seven quarters, $165M+ and growing rapidly. Their healthcare clients are already having AI agents call each other over phone lines in English — a delightful absurdity that reinforces the principle that existing rails (telephone networks, natural language) often beat fancy new protocols. Sierra's **outcome-based pricing** model (charging only for resolved cases, not token usage) is presented as a fundamental shift comparable to the move from impression-based to CPC advertising.

Taylor offers a **nuanced view on the SaaS correction**, arguing the market selloff is rational but overblown — it reflects genuine uncertainty about whether AI agents will erode the value of traditional systems of record, particularly those that are "systems of engagement" rather than true ledgers. He predicts the gravitational center of enterprise value may shift from databases to the encoded, optimized processes that agents represent.

Looking ahead, Taylor predicts 2026 will bring **a meaningful AI-driven scientific breakthrough** that captures mainstream attention, continued explosive growth in agent adoption, and the possibility that we may eventually see the end of the smartphone era — replaced by conversational interfaces that free us from screen addiction.

## Top 25 Takeaways

### 1. Coding Agents Have Undergone a Radical Transformation
The difference between coding agents in October 2025 and March 2026 is so stark that the entire conversation about software engineering's future would be "materially different." Taylor says most Silicon Valley companies won't write code by hand by end of 2026.

### 2. The Atomic Unit of AI Productivity Is a Process, Not a Person
Taylor's core framework: AI doesn't replace people — it optimizes processes. A person's job spans physical and digital tasks; AI only excels at the digital. Companies should identify end-to-end business processes (like supplier onboarding) and optimize those, not try to automate roles.

### 3. Code Repos Are Uniquely Suited to AI — Don't Extrapolate Linearly
Code bases have all context in one place (textual files), built-in feedback loops (compilers, tests, code review), and formal change histories. Most business domains lack these qualities, so the straight-line extrapolation from "AI is great at coding" to "AI will do everything" is flawed.

### 4. Documentation May Become More Valuable Than Code
Taylor hypothesizes that a coding session should produce a documentation artifact (the "product manager output") alongside code (the "engineer output"). The documentation — capturing intent, PRDs, and customer problems — may be the more durable asset. The great irony: engineers may spend most of their time writing docs.

### 5. The Rise of the Hyper-Generalist
A new archetype is emerging: people who combine taste, customer empathy, infrastructure understanding, and high agency but aren't necessarily the deepest specialist in any one area. These "hyper-generalists" were historically sidelined in large orgs but are now "massively ascendant" because AI gives them the exoskeleton to execute on their ideas.

### 6. MCP Is Overrated — Rich Context Beats Thin Protocols
Taylor is "growing more skeptical of MCP as a meaningful part of the future." The OpenClaw approach of writing everything to markdown files produces more useful agents than architectures with many specialized sub-agents connected via thin protocols. Agents need far more context than MCP affords.

### 7. Multi-Agent Architectures Look Good on Whiteboards but Often Fail
The vision of specialized sub-agents (fraud detection, personalization, etc.) orchestrated by a super-agent "looks really good on a whiteboard" but tends to produce robotic experiences because all context gets stuffed into sub-agents while the orchestrating agent has no real understanding.

### 8. Memory Is the Key Missing Ingredient in Consumer AI
In 2026, mainstream AI products like ChatGPT and Gemini still start as blank slates with no memory. OpenClaw's janky markdown-file memory system, despite its bugs, produces a more compelling agent experience than polished products without persistent context.

### 9. Agent Harnesses > APIs
Taylor envisions a future where software companies don't just offer APIs but provide full "agent harnesses" — documentation, rules, skills, and context that encode the expertise of the best possible user. "An API is the button you click. A harness is the instruction manual for all the Unix commands that power Stripe."

### 10. Outcome-Based Pricing Is the Future of Software
Sierra charges only when an AI agent successfully resolves a case — not per token or per seat. Taylor draws the analogy to the shift from impression-based to CPC advertising: charging closer to business value is more efficient for everyone and creates genuine alignment between vendor and customer.

### 11. Outcome-Based ≠ Usage-Based
Taylor draws a sharp distinction: usage-based pricing (tokens, storage) has no guaranteed correlation with value delivered. An agent that uses 1/100th the tokens but produces 1/10th the sales is still worse. Outcomes — resolved cases, sales commissions, reduced churn — are what matter.

### 12. Sierra Is Growing at Extraordinary Speed
$100M ARR in seven quarters, $150M in eight, ~$165M one month into the next quarter. Over half of clients have $1B+ revenue; about a third exceed $10B. The company is two years old.

### 13. AI Agents Are Already Calling Each Other Over Phone Lines
Sierra's healthcare clients have AI agents on both sides of phone calls — insurance payers answering and healthcare providers calling — conducting business in English over the public switched telephone network. "TCP/IP and English over PSTN" — existing rails beat fancy new protocols.

### 14. The SaaS Correction Is Rational but Overblown
The 20-30% decline in SaaS valuations reflects genuine uncertainty: the annuity model of software-as-a-service depends on durable recurring revenue, and if AI agents start performing the labor that software supported, that annuity may not be as durable. But it's an indictment of the sector, not individual companies — any of them could pull a Microsoft-style reinvention.

### 15. Systems of Record vs. Systems of Engagement
Taylor's framework for evaluating SaaS durability: the closer a product is to a true system of record (like a financial ledger), the more durable its value. The closer it is to a "system of engagement," the more vulnerable it is to disruption by AI agents that can perform the work directly.

### 16. AI Agents Will Become a Company's Primary Digital Interface
Taylor believes a company's AI agent — singular, branded — will become the dominant digital interface for customer interactions, spanning chat, phone, WhatsApp, and potentially smart speakers. This transforms customer service from a cost center to a strategic asset.

### 17. The Jevons Paradox Is Already Playing Out
One Sierra retailer's total customer conversation volume went up nearly as much as the AI-driven savings — because replacing terrible chatbots with delightful AI agents made customers *want* to interact more. Cost didn't drop much, but engagement and customer listening exploded.

### 18. Supervisor Models Solve the Hallucination Problem
Sierra uses a "constellation of models" where supervisor agents inspect the reasoning of the primary agent and send it back with notes if it goes off-script. Chaining two 90%-accurate systems yields ~99% effectiveness — layering compute and reasoning creates production-grade reliability.

### 19. Well-Known Brands Are Harder to Ground Than Obscure Ones
Counter-intuitively, making a grounded AI agent for a well-known brand is harder than for an obscure one, because the LLM's pretrained knowledge creates "temptation to go off-script" — the model thinks it knows the answer when it should look it up.

### 20. AI Is a Competitive Imperative, Not a Competitive Advantage
Like websites in 1994, AI adoption is something companies must do to stay relevant, not something that provides lasting differentiation. The more interesting question is the second- and third-order effects when *every* competitor in an industry has adopted the same technology.

### 21. Companies Are "Shipping Their Org Charts" — That's the Real Blocker
The biggest impediment to AI productivity isn't technology but organizational structure. Companies optimize by department (legal, procurement, IT) rather than by process. There's rarely a person accountable for an end-to-end process like supplier onboarding, so AI gets applied in silos rather than transformatively.

### 22. The Applied AI Market Is Dangerously Immature
Taylor argues that the lack of mature, purpose-built AI solutions for specific business processes is one of the main factors impeding AI adoption. Too many SF startups build "rote tools around AI" rather than agents for boring-but-valuable business processes. This immaturity is holding back trillions in economic value.

### 23. The Smartphone Era May Eventually End
Taylor speculates that conversational AI interfaces could eventually unwedge us from addictive screen-based interactions. The ingredients now exist for a post-smartphone paradigm — not imminent, but the technology affords that kind of innovation for the first time.

### 24. Taylor's 2026 Predictions
- A meaningful AI-driven scientific breakthrough will capture mainstream attention (beyond incomprehensible math papers)
- Mainstream adoption of AI agents by consumers and companies accelerates dramatically
- Most Silicon Valley companies stop writing code by hand
- OpenClaw-style personal agents go from niche community to mainstream

### 25. OpenAI Board: A Fiduciary Duty to a Mission
Taylor describes the unique experience of having a fiduciary duty not to shareholders but to the mission of "ensuring AGI benefits humanity." This framing profoundly changes board-level decision-making. He also built the board essentially from scratch after the Sam Altman crisis, carefully designing composition around safety, economics, infrastructure, and the non-profit mission.
